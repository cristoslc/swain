# Task Switching: Cognitive Costs of Context Shifts

## Core Finding

- Monsell 2003: responses are substantially slower and more error-prone immediately after a task switch
- Switch cost is measurable and consistent across experimental paradigms
- Context switching imposes significant cognitive penalties on both productivity and error rates

## Nature of Switch Cost

- Switch cost results from both transient and long-term carry-over of task-set activation and inhibition
- Two contributing mechanisms:
  - Proactive control: deliberate task-set preparation (reconfiguring goals, attention, response mappings)
  - Automatic inertia effects: residual activation from the prior task interferes with the new one
- Preparation reduces but does not eliminate switch cost — a residual cost remains even after approximately 600ms of preparation time

## Implications for Decision Work

- Each shift between decision types requires the brain to reload relevant information and adjust its processing mode
- The transition cost depletes cognitive resources without producing any decision output — it is pure overhead
- Frequent switching between unrelated decision domains compounds the cost multiplicatively
- Batching similar decisions reduces the number of switches and preserves cognitive resources for actual deliberation
