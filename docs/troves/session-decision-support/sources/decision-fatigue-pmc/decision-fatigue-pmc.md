# Decision Fatigue and the Strength Model of Self-Control

## Theoretical Foundations

- Decision fatigue derives from Baumeister's Strength Model of Self-Control
- Ego depletion: humans have a limited capacity to regulate behavior, akin to muscle fatigue — exerting self-control on one task diminishes the resource available for subsequent tasks
- Alternative framework: the Process Model attributes fatigue not to resource depletion but to alterations in attentional regulation and motivation

## Sequential Decision Degradation

- Judges' parole approval rates decline significantly after lunch — early-morning and post-break decisions are more favorable
- Physicians prescribe unnecessary antibiotics at higher rates as the clinical day progresses
- Decision quality degrades as a function of cumulative decisions, not merely elapsed time

## Behavioral Manifestations

- Decision-fatigued individuals exhibit:
  - Procrastination — deferring decisions to avoid cognitive cost
  - Passivity — accepting default options rather than actively choosing
  - Impulsivity — making hasty choices to escape the burden of deliberation
  - Default-option reliance — gravitating toward status quo or pre-selected alternatives

## Gaps in the Literature

- No specific optimal decision count thresholds have been established — the literature does not define a "safe" number of daily decisions
- No empirically validated interventions are documented in this review
- The widely cited "35,000 decisions a day" statistic is mentioned without establishing safe ranges or methodological grounding
