# The Neuroscience of Decision Fatigue and Decision Batching

## Decision Batching as Mitigation

- Batching similar decisions together reduces costly cognitive switching
- When grouping related decisions, the brain enters a specific problem-solving mode and operates more efficiently within that mode
- The efficiency gain comes from avoiding repeated task-set reconfiguration

## The Cost of Context Switching

- Context switching is expensive: each shift requires reloading relevant information and adjusting the cognitive processing mode
- The transition itself produces no decision output — it is pure overhead
- Frequent switching fragments attention and accelerates depletion of executive function resources

## Neurological Basis

- Prolonged cognitive load reduces glucose availability in the prefrontal cortex, impairing executive functions
- The prefrontal cortex is the seat of deliberative decision-making, planning, and impulse control
- As prefrontal resources deplete, decision-making shifts toward habitual and default-driven responses

## Temporal and Structural Strategies

- Scheduling critical decisions during biological cognitive peaks (typically morning hours) improves decision quality
- Rational agents with limited resources should consider not just data fit but the cognitive complexity of representations
- Simpler mental models that sacrifice marginal accuracy may outperform complex ones when cognitive resources are constrained
