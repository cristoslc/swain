# The Zeigarnik Effect: Cognitive Tension from Incomplete Tasks

## Core Phenomenon

- People remember unfinished or interrupted tasks better than completed ones
- Recall ratio approximately 1.9 — nearly twice as many interrupted tasks are remembered compared to completed tasks
- Named after Bluma Zeigarnik, who observed the effect in the 1920s

## Mechanism

- Starting a task establishes task-specific tension, grounded in Lewin's field theory of motivation
- This tension persists until the task reaches completion, maintaining the task in active memory
- Connected to the Gestalt law of closure: humans have an intrinsic desire for wholeness and completeness

## Individual Differences

- Ambitious people are more affected — they show higher recall of incomplete tasks than less motivated individuals
- The effect is modulated by personal investment in the task outcome

## Design Implications

- Progress bars capitalize on the Zeigarnik effect — showing an incomplete state maintains cognitive tension and drives completion behavior
- Unbounded work creates persistent cognitive tension because there is no clear completion signal to release it
- Explicit completion signals (checkmarks, status transitions, "done" confirmations) relieve the accumulated tension
- Without deliberate boundaries, open tasks accumulate as background cognitive load
