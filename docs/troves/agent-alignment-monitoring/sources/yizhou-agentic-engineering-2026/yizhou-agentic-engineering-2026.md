# 2025 Overpromised AI Agents. 2026 Demands Agentic Engineering.

**Author:** Yi Zhou (Award-Winning CTO & CIO, AI Thought Leader, LinkedIn Top Voice)
**Published:** January 2, 2026
**Publication:** Agentic AI & GenAI Revolution (Medium)

## Core Thesis

Autonomy was overpromised in 2025 and under-engineered. The problem was never intelligence — it was the absence of engineering discipline around autonomous systems.

## Key Arguments (from search snippets)

### Agents ran without executable governance

> "Agents ran without executable governance. Policies lived in documents and slide decks, not in code that could constrain behavior at runtime. Once an agent began acting, intent dissolved into best-effort suggestions."

This is the central insight: governance as documentation vs. governance as executable constraint. When governance lives only in documents (AGENTS.md, policy PDFs, slide decks), agents treat it as advisory. The moment an agent begins acting, the governance layer has no enforcement mechanism.

### No observability of reasoning

> "They operated without observability of reasoning. Logs captured outputs, but not decisions. Drift accumulated invisibly until failure forced investigation — often too late."

Decision observability — not just output logging — is essential for detecting process misalignment. Without reasoning traces, drift accumulates silently.

### The engineering gap

> "Across industries, 90-95% of AI initiatives still fail to reach sustained production value, and fewer than 12% deliver measurable ROI — not because models are weak, but because teams lack an engineering discipline for autonomous systems."

> "Those failures surface as silent drift, governance gaps, post-incident confusion, and expensive redesigns."

### Building agents without rigor is no longer a technical oversight

> "By 2026, building agents without this rigor is no longer a technical oversight."

The article argues for codifying what it takes to move from "impressive agent demos to systems that can be trusted in production — across security, observability, governance, memory, reasoning, and orchestration."
