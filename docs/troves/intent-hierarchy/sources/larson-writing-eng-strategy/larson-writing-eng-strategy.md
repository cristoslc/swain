# Writing an Engineering Strategy

**Published:** February 13, 2023
**Author:** Will Larson

## Overview

This article, an unedited chapter from "The Engineering Executive's Primer," explores how engineering executives can develop and communicate an effective engineering strategy. The author draws on Richard Rumelt's framework from "Good Strategy, Bad Strategy" to define strategy as comprising three essential components: diagnosis, guiding policies, and coherent actions.

## Key Concepts

### Rumelt's Strategy Framework

The author emphasizes that "a strategy is composed of three parts: diagnosis (a theory describing the nature of the challenge), guiding policies (approaches you'll apply to grapple with the challenge), and coherent actions (specific actions directed by guiding policy)."

A strategy must address real problems through actionable policies. Visions of ideal states without grounding in current reality often fail because they don't help organizations navigate actual challenges.

### Why Strategy Often Goes Undocumented

The author notes that many organizations lack written engineering strategies, not because strategy doesn't exist, but because executives fail to recognize that their implicit approach to problem-solving *is* their strategy. Documentation makes implicit decisions explicit, enabling better enforcement and organizational alignment.

## Writing Process for Executives

The recommended process involves ten steps:

1. **Commit to writing it yourself** rather than delegating, as you possess unique perspective
2. **Identify full stakeholder set** including executives, senior engineers, and managers
3. **Select a working group** of 3-5 people for early feedback
4. **Draft the diagnosis** incorporating roadmap, competitive pressures, financial plans, and survey data
5. **Write guiding policies** with working group feedback plus external executive review
6. **Share with full stakeholders** (expect it to leak company-wide)
7. **Draft coherent actions** connecting specific initiatives to guiding policies
8. **Meet 1:1 with potential dissenters** to ensure they feel heard
9. **Share with full organization** with a short feedback window (approximately one week)
10. **Finalize and commit to reviewing impact** within two months

### Three Core Questions for Guiding Policies

Effective strategies answer:

1. **Resource allocation:** How are engineering resources distributed across priorities, and why?
2. **Fundamental rules:** What consistent standards must all teams follow (technology stack, processes)?
3. **Decision-making:** How are decisions made, and how do teams escalate when needed?

## Evaluating Guiding Policies

Policies should be:

- **Applicable:** Usable for navigating real tradeoffs and complex decisions
- **Enforced:** Teams held accountable for following them consistently
- **Creating leverage:** Producing compounding or multiplicative impact across the organization

The author cautions that "many forms of leverage are accessible to teams within engineering, and it's often not necessary to directly address those opportunities in your engineering strategy." Strategy operates most effectively at an altitude where organizational-level enforcement is required.

## When to Write Strategy

Three prerequisites exist:

1. Confidence in your diagnosis or trust in the organization to inform it
2. Willingness and ability to enforce the strategy
3. At least one year's conviction that the strategy will create value

## Addressing Missing Company Strategies

Since engineering strategy intersects with business and product strategies, executives often must privately draft missing strategies from other functions. The author recommends exploring questions about cash flow targets, investment thesis, business unit structure, user needs, and competitive threats -- without publicly undermining responsible executives.

## Coherent Actions Categories

Three types of actions implement guiding policies:

- **Enforcements:** Processes maintaining rules and evaluating exceptions
- **Escalations:** Constructive pathways for challenging policies that don't apply
- **Transitions:** Moving from current state to new state (reorganizations, migrations)

## On Bottoms-Up Strategy

The author addresses criticism that top-down strategy removes autonomy. Strategy cannot function as pure bottoms-up because enforcement -- the mechanism that makes strategy effective -- requires top-down authority. The real concern usually isn't about top-down versus bottoms-up, but whether specific strategies are well-considered or whether decision-making processes were inclusive.

## Practical Example

The article includes a complete example strategy addressing:
- Business line revenue distribution
- Organizational sizing constraints
- Developer productivity challenges (test flakiness)
- Communication gaps across teams

Its guiding policies cover resource ratios, staffing allocation, cash-flow neutrality, technology standardization, escalation processes, and communication channels.

## Starting Point

The author emphasizes that perfect strategies are unnecessary. Beginning by documenting existing implicit strategies creates organizational pull toward improvement. "It's better to go slowly than to avoid starting altogether."
