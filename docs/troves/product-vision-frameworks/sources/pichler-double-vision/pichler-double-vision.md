# Double Vision: Two Approaches (Roman Pichler)

## Option 1: Vision as Strategic Positioning (Geoffrey Moore)
Template: "For (target customers) who are dissatisfied with (current alternative), our product is a (category) that (key benefit). Unlike (competition), our product (key difference)."

- Effectively positions the product
- Describes WHO it's for and HOW it differs
- Limitation: describes positioning, not purpose

## Option 2: Vision as Big Aspirational Goal (Pichler's recommendation)
Example: For a diabetes management app, the vision is simply: "HEALTHY EATING"

### Six Quality Criteria
1. **Inspiring** — resonates with stakeholders and teams
2. **Shared** — individuals genuinely support it
3. **Ethical** — creates no harm to people or planet
4. **Concise** — memorable statement or slogan
5. **Ambitious** — a big, audacious goal possibly never fully achieved
6. **Enduring** — provides guidance for at least five years

## Product Vision Board
Combines aspirational vision with strategy:
- Top: Product vision (the aspirational goal)
- Bottom four sections: Target group, Needs, Product, Business goals

## Why Option 2 Is Better
1. **Explicit purpose** — the WHY is clearly stated, creating alignment during difficult periods
2. **Separation from strategy** — vision remains stable while strategy evolves. Could pivot from app to book and still pursue "healthy eating"
3. **Clarity** — visual templates easier to understand than text-heavy statements

## Key Insight
Vision and strategy should be "loosely coupled." Vision provides enduring direction; strategy remains changeable.
