# Tenets: Definition, Purpose, and Practice (Charlie Kindel)

## Definition

"Tenets are a few carefully articulated guiding principles for any endeavor. They act as a guide to align on a vision and simplify decision-making."

Tenets function as written-down rules that distinguish welcome behavior from unacceptable behavior. They define culture and drive individual behaviors.

## Two Types

**Foundational tenets** — describe why a team or product exists and its intended customer value.

**Aspirational tenets** — describe how a team or product intends to operate, even if not currently doing so.

## Key Principles for Writing

1. **Obsess over Customers** — at least one tenet should describe endeavor-specific customer value delivery
2. **Be Memorable** — concise and challenging to the reader
3. **Be Endeavor-Specific** — specific enough to excite the team and surprise outsiders. Generic tenets communicate virtually no useful information
4. **Counsel, Don't Prescribe** — guide through trade-offs by taking a stand that one thing matters more than another
5. **One Main Idea Per Tenet** — "a single essential idea," making it both memorable and clear
6. **Minimal Coverage** — cover the semantic space using the minimum number necessary
7. **Long-Term Durability** — strategic and durable, surviving multiple rounds of goal-setting
8. **Present Tense** — "will" or "should" is almost always a mistake; tenets are present tense
9. **Distinguish Rather Than Elevate** — capture what makes a team different, not what makes it superior

## "Unless You Know Better Ones"

Every set of tenets at Amazon is followed by "unless you know better ones." This reinforces that tenets evolve. Teams should improve tenets through input and learning.

"You can never spend enough time debating the tenets for a program." — Jeff Bezos

## Why Write Tenets?

1. **Cultural Foundation** — intentional, directed culture rather than random emergence
2. **Agreement on Unprovables** — resolve debates on questions that cannot be answered with data
3. **Detection of Dishonesty** — expose groupthink and maintain strategic focus

## Relationship to Strategy

Tenets are aligned with mission and core values while being specific to a particular endeavor. They appear at the beginning of narratives to ensure consistency. They constitute the "Principle" component of planning frameworks.

## Examples

- "Our Products Make Other Products Fantastic" — value lies in integration, not standalone use
- "We Stand on the Shoulders of Giants" — favor adaptation over invention
- "Our Org Structure is Invisible to Customers" — unified entity despite internal structures
- "On-Premise Compute Comes First" — mission-critical needs on-premise
- "Defense is the Best Offense" — patents serve primarily as defense
- "Get Data and Use Data" — invest in measurement when data unavailable
