# Stacey Matrix — Praxis Framework

The Stacey Matrix was developed and published by Ralph Douglas Stacey. It is designed to help understand the factors that contribute to complexity and choose the best management actions to address different degrees of complexity.

## Two Dimensions

The basis of the matrix is two dimensions: **agreement** and **certainty**.

**Certainty axis:** Projects are close to certainty when cause and effect relationships are well known and similar projects have been performed in the past. It is then usually possible to extrapolate from past experience. Projects far from certainty deliver something new and innovative — cause and effect relationships are unclear and past experience is of little help.

**Agreement axis:** Members of a group, team or organisation will have differing views on the objectives and how to achieve them. Governance, management style and approach depend upon the level of agreement.

## Five Zones

### Zone 1: Close to agreement, close to certainty
Data can be gathered from the past to predict the future. Work is controlled by monitoring against detailed plans. Construction and engineering projects typically fall here.

### Zone 2: Far from agreement, close to certainty
Projects are very certain about which objectives are possible but there is less agreement about which objectives are of greatest value. Skills such as negotiation, compromise and coalition-building are important. Decision making becomes political rather than technical.

### Zone 3: Close to agreement, far from certainty
High agreement about desired objectives but not much certainty about cause and effect linkages. A strong shared vision is necessary and a flexible, realistic approach to planning. Must visibly head towards an agreed future state even though specific paths are not fully predetermined.

### Zone 4: The zone of complexity
Low levels of agreement or certainty make the project a complex management problem. Often triggers poor decision-making practices when it really needs high levels of creativity, innovation and freedom from past constraints. Adaptability and agility are key skills.

### Zone 5: Far from agreement, far from certainty
Anarchy/chaos can prevail. The boundary between this and the zone of complexity is the "Edge of chaos." Strategies are needed to address these situations.

## Relationship to Cynefin

The Stacey matrix has a lot in common with other complexity-based models. The Cynefin Framework helps understand how to respond to simple, complicated, complex and chaotic circumstances. Praxis maps these four Cynefin areas onto the Stacey Matrix zones:
- Zone 1 → Simple/Clear
- Zone 2/3 → Complicated
- Zone 4 → Complex
- Zone 5 → Chaotic

Source: Stacey RD. *Strategic management and organisational dynamics: the challenge of complexity*. 3rd ed. Harlow: Prentice Hall, 2002.
