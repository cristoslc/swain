# Understanding The Stacey Matrix & How It Helps Manage Complexities

By Jill Romford, AgilityPortal. Feb 17, 2026.

## Definition and Origins

The Stacey Matrix, developed by Professor Ralph Stacey in the early 1990s, is a framework designed to aid in the understanding and management of organizational complexity. It shifted the focus from traditional linear management models to ones that accommodate ambiguity and unpredictability.

## Components

The matrix comprises two critical components: the axes of **certainty** and **agreement**.

- **Certainty axis**: extent to which an organization can predict outcomes
- **Agreement axis**: level of consensus among stakeholders

These axes intersect to form four quadrants: simple, complicated, complex, and chaotic.

## Four Zones

### Simple Zone
High certainty, high agreement. Problems are well-understood, predictable, routine. Solutions are straightforward — standard operating procedures. Management focused on efficiency and consistency.

### Complicated Zone
High certainty, lower agreement. Requires specialized knowledge or expertise. Multiple variables or sophisticated processes. Decision-making relies on expertise, data analysis, structured methodologies.

### Complex Zone
Low certainty, low agreement. Dynamic, unpredictable, no clear solutions. Requires innovative thinking and adaptive approaches. Leadership involves facilitating dialogue, exploring perspectives, experimenting.

### Chaotic Zone
Highly uncertain, no agreement. Crisis scenarios. Immediate action needed. Traditional approaches may not apply. Focus on stabilizing, then move to another zone.

## Advantages for Process Selection

- **Visual simplicity**: quickly hand-drawn for stakeholder comprehension
- **Early project evaluation**: assess before commencement
- **Proactive issue revelation**: surfaces challenges and responsibilities early
- **Structured decision-making**: "What" and "How" division provides clear argumentation structure

## Disadvantages

- Does not offer specific approaches to address the identified problem
- Subjective: varying experience leads to disparate assessments
- Serves as a model lacking real-world conditions (resources, time)
- Risk of instrumentalization: shaping project to fit a preconceived approach

## Application in Agile

The matrix helps Agile teams categorize projects and select practices:
- Simple → straightforward Agile practices
- Complicated → more structured Scrum approach
- Complex → adaptive Kanban style, iterative approach
- Chaotic → rapid responses and quick pivots

Categorization can change over time, necessitating continuous reassessment.

## Key Takeaway for Automated Process Decisions

The matrix's ability to guide teams in choosing the most effective practices based on specific project characteristics directly supports automated ceremony selection — score certainty and agreement, map to a zone, apply the corresponding process intensity.
